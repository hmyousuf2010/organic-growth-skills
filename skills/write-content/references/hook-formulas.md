# Hook Formulas

The hook is the first line people see. It determines whether they read the rest or scroll past. These formulas are proven across millions of tweets.

## The 8 hook types

### 1. Curiosity gap
Reveal the beginning and the end, but omit the middle. The reader has to click/read to fill the gap.

"When Iron Man came out, RDJ was paid $500K. He's since earned $450M+. Here's how:"
"I went from 0 to 10K followers in 57 days. No threads. No viral tweets. Here's what I did instead."

### 2. Contrarian
Challenge something everyone believes. The stronger the disagreement, the more replies.

"College completely failed in teaching me how to write."
"You don't need a morning routine. You need fewer obligations."
"React is holding the web back."

### 3. Number/stat
Lead with a surprising, specific data point. Specificity is what makes it hook — "99%" is better than "most."

"99% of business books are 1 idea stretched across 300 pages."
"I analyzed 10,000 tweets. Only 3% had external links. Here's why."

### 4. Story
Transport the reader to a specific moment. Time + place + emotion.

"In 2019, I was broke with $400 in my bank account."
"Last Tuesday at 2am I got a Slack message that changed everything."

### 5. Question
Expose a knowledge gap the reader wants filled. Rhetorical or direct.

"Why do some threads get millions of views while yours struggle to hit 1,000?"
"What's the one thing every successful founder does that nobody talks about?"

### 6. Bold claim
Make a statement so strong that people have to respond — either to agree passionately or to argue.

"Everything you've been told about building an audience is wrong."
"The best code is the code you don't write."

### 7. Credibility + promise
Establish authority first, then promise specific value. The credibility makes the promise believable.

"Over 10 years, I've read 500+ business books. I distilled the best into 10:"
"I've hired 200+ engineers. Here's the one question that predicts success:"

### 8. Audience call-out
Name the exact audience in the first line. If they identify, they read. Everyone else scrolls — but that's fine because you get concentrated engagement from your target.

"If you're a developer with under 1K followers, read this:"
"Startup founders: If you're still doing your own bookkeeping, stop."

## Hook rules

1. The hook must work as a standalone statement. If the thread disappears, the hook should still be interesting.
2. Front-load the most important words. On mobile, only the first 40-50 characters are visible in the timeline.
3. Don't waste the hook on throat-clearing. No "I've been thinking about..." or "So I wanted to share..." Get to the point.
4. Specific numbers beat vague claims. "$14K MRR" beats "significant revenue." "57 days" beats "quickly."
5. The hook should create tension that the thread resolves. If there's no tension, there's no reason to keep reading.

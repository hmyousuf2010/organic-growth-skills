# Sector-Specific Content Profiles

Audience profiles and content patterns for key sectors on Twitter/X and GitHub.

## Dev Twitter

**Audience:** Software engineers, indie hackers, open source maintainers, DevRel professionals.

**What performs well:**
- Shipping updates with technical detail
- Debugging war stories (relatable pain)
- Hot takes on frameworks and tools
- "TIL" posts with genuinely surprising discoveries
- Tool recommendations with specific use cases
- Open source announcements with demo GIFs
- Code screenshots of elegant solutions
- Performance benchmarks and comparisons

**Writing style:** Dry wit, self-deprecating humor, technically precise yet casual. Anti-corporate. Direct.

**Example:** "Spent 6 hours debugging. The issue? A missing semicolon. I love this profession."

**Key accounts to study:** Follow accounts in the user's specific tech stack. Rust Twitter, React Twitter, Go Twitter, etc. are distinct subcultures.

**Content gaps:** Most dev content is either too promotional (company blog energy) or too niche (only 50 people understand it). The sweet spot is technical insights explained accessibly with personality.

## Crypto Twitter (CT)

**Audience:** Traders, DeFi users, protocol developers, NFT collectors, degens, researchers.

**What performs well:**
- Alpha threads (genuine research, not shilling)
- Market analysis with charts
- Protocol deep dives
- Meme commentary on market movements
- "gm" posts with value attached
- Airdrop and farming strategy threads
- Narrative plays and macro analysis

**Writing style:** High-energy, meme-heavy, tribal, urgent. GM/GN rituals. .eth handles. Terse. Heavy irony and in-jokes.

**Example:** "gm. shipped a new feature overnight. wagmi."

**Key vocabulary:** gm, gn, wagmi, ngmi, ser, anon, fren, wen, LFG, DYOR, NFA, HODL, diamond hands, paper hands, ape in, rekt, degen, alpha, cope, rug, ATH, "probably nothing," "few"

**Content gaps:** Most CT content is low-effort shilling or recycled takes. Genuine technical analysis, honest assessments, and educational content stand out.

## Startup Twitter

**Audience:** Founders, VCs, angel investors, early employees, indie hackers.

**What performs well:**
- Build-in-public updates with real metrics
- Failure stories with honest lessons
- Milestone celebrations (revenue, users, funding)
- Hiring posts framed as opportunities not job listings
- Fundraising journey transparency
- Product launches with specific numbers
- Contrarian takes on startup advice

**Writing style:** Build-in-public voice. Monthly MRR updates. Rejection stories turned lessons. Vulnerability combined with hustle.

**Example:** "Month 6: $8K MRR to $14K MRR. What changed? We stopped building features nobody asked for."

**Content gaps:** Too many founders share generic "10 lessons" threads. What works is specificity — actual numbers, actual mistakes, actual pivots with reasoning.

## AI/ML Twitter

**Audience:** ML researchers, AI engineers, prompt engineers, AI product builders, enthusiasts.

**What performs well:**
- Paper summaries with accessible TL;DRs
- Demo videos of new capabilities
- Benchmark comparisons and analysis
- Hot takes on model releases
- "Just dropped" energy for releases and tools
- Tutorial threads on using new models
- Comparison threads (Model A vs B for task X)

**Writing style:** Mix of technical depth and casual energy. Paper citations but explained plainly. Demo-first approach.

**Example:** "New paper from DeepMind. They achieved SOTA on [benchmark] by [method]. The key insight: [one-liner]."

**Content gaps:** Most AI Twitter is either too hype-driven ("this changes EVERYTHING") or too academic. Practical insights on using AI tools effectively are underserved.

## Design Twitter

**Audience:** UI/UX designers, product designers, design engineers, creative directors.

**What performs well:**
- Before/after design reveals
- Process breakdowns (how the sausage is made)
- Design critique threads
- Tool and plugin comparisons
- Micro-interaction showcases (video required)
- Design system documentation
- "Spot the difference" challenges

**Writing style:** Visual-first (screenshots and videos mandatory). Process-oriented. Opinionated about craft. Attention to detail is the flex.

**Example:** "The difference between good and great UI is 4px of padding. I'm not joking."

**Content gaps:** Most design content is portfolio-style showcase. What's underserved is the thinking behind decisions — why this layout, why this color, why this interaction pattern.

## Cross-sector patterns

Regardless of sector, these patterns consistently perform:

1. **Specificity beats generality.** "$8K to $14K MRR" beats "grew revenue significantly."
2. **Vulnerability beats polish.** "We almost died in Month 2" beats "Our journey to success."
3. **Teaching beats telling.** Show the how, not just the what.
4. **Opinions beat neutrality.** Take a stance. People engage with takes, not summaries.
5. **Visual beats text-only.** Add a screenshot, GIF, chart, or code snippet when possible.
